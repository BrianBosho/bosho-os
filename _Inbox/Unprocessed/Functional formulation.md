A psychotherapist would probably describe this less as “which kleśa do you have?” and more as a **functional formulation**:

> What is happening internally, what triggers it, what maintains it, and what function does the apparent indifference serve?

I would not diagnose you from a brain dump. But the psychological vocabulary that fits best is something like this.

## 1. Role overload and cognitive overload

You are carrying several roles simultaneously:

- paper author;
    
- research lead;
    
- collaborator;
    
- future PhD student;
    
- immigration/logistics manager;
    
- major-life-decision maker;
    
- prospective entrepreneur.
    

Each role produces different priorities and deadlines. The mind must continually remember, compare, sequence, and inhibit them. That creates **cognitive overload** and **role overload**.

The opposite is not “become more motivated.” It is **externalized structure and role containment**:

> From 8–11, I am only the Fedprop author.  
> In the evening logistics block, I am only handling the PhD transition.

This is one reason your fixed blocks feel relieving: they temporarily remove competing identities and decisions.

## 2. Threat appraisal and anticipatory anxiety

Your mind is treating many unresolved matters as potential future threats:

- “What if the visa is delayed?”
    
- “What if I choose the wrong starting location?”
    
- “What if the paper never gets finished?”
    
- “What if I disappoint the team?”
    
- “What if this says something serious about my capacity?”
    

This is **anticipatory anxiety**: anxiety generated not by a present catastrophe, but by uncertain possible outcomes. Uncertainty is especially disruptive when the mind estimates both the likelihood and the cost of negative outcomes as high. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4276319/?utm_source=chatgpt.com "Uncertainty and Anticipation in Anxiety: An integrated neurobiological and psychological perspective - PMC"))

The opposite is **realistic threat appraisal**:

> What exactly is the threat?  
> How likely is it?  
> What action reduces it?  
> What part cannot be controlled today?

For example, the visa problem is not “my future is in danger.” It is: “I have an August 18 appointment and need to investigate whether an earlier interview can be requested.”

## 3. Intolerance of uncertainty

This may be particularly important in the Portugal decision.

You seem to want enough certainty to know that Portugal or Pittsburgh is the _right_ beginning. But the decision contains unavoidable unknowns: supervisor fit, research direction, culture, finances, coursework, recovery, and later opportunities.

**Intolerance of uncertainty** does not mean disliking uncertainty—almost everyone does. It means that uncertainty itself becomes so aversive that the person repeatedly analyzes, delays, seeks more information, or struggles to commit. It is studied as a process that cuts across several anxiety presentations. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7704173/?utm_source=chatgpt.com "From Uncertainty to Anxiety: How Uncertainty Fuels Anxiety in a Process Mediated by Intolerance of Uncertainty - PMC"))

Its opposite is **uncertainty tolerance**:

> “I will collect the decision-relevant information, establish a deadline, and then choose without requiring complete certainty.”

## 4. Experiential avoidance

Opening Fedprop does not only expose you to technical work. It exposes you to:

- guilt;
    
- disappointment;
    
- fear of seeing how much remains;
    
- self-criticism;
    
- stale boredom;
    
- possible judgment from collaborators.
    

So lying down, disengaging, planning indefinitely, or switching toward a novel app idea may sometimes function as **experiential avoidance**—moving away from an activity partly to avoid the thoughts, emotions, and bodily sensations it evokes.

Experiential avoidance means trying to escape, suppress, or alter difficult internal experiences, particularly when this produces short-term relief but longer-term costs. In ACT, its alternative is psychological flexibility: remaining open to discomfort while taking values-directed action. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12939156/?utm_source=chatgpt.com "Experiential Avoidance and Psychoactive Substance Use: Systematic Review - PMC"))

The opposite is **willingness**:

> “I am willing to feel guilt, uncertainty, and resistance while rewriting this one table.”

Not excitement. Not emotional purification before work. Willingness.

## 5. Avoidance learning and task aversiveness

Fedprop has probably become **conditioned as aversive**.

Each cycle may have gone:

1. You think about Fedprop.
    
2. Pressure, guilt, and bodily tension arise.
    
3. You postpone or disengage.
    
4. The pressure temporarily reduces.
    
5. The mind learns that avoidance provides relief.
    
6. The unfinished task becomes more threatening next time.
    

This is a **negative reinforcement loop**: avoidance is strengthened because it removes discomfort in the short term.

The opposite is **graded approach** or exposure:

- open the paper;
    
- work on a tightly bounded unit;
    
- tolerate the activation;
    
- finish at a defined stopping point;
    
- repeat until contact with the paper becomes less threatening.
    

Your repeated 8–11 block can do this, provided each session is bounded and not treated as an ordeal you must survive.

## 6. Maladaptive perfectionism and overcontrol

Your standards are not simply “do good work.” They sometimes become:

- make the best PhD choice;
    
- use every opportunity well;
    
- make the paper comprehensive;
    
- do not waste time;
    
- lead the team properly;
    
- know exactly what should happen next;
    
- feel appropriately excited about valuable work.
    

That last one matters: you are even evaluating whether you are having the **correct emotion**.

A perfectionistic system often combines high standards with harsh self-evaluation and difficulty tolerating incomplete, ambiguous, or merely adequate work. Perfectionism and procrastination frequently occur together, especially when work is evaluative and mistakes feel personally threatening. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8542725/?utm_source=chatgpt.com "Procrastination, Perfectionism, and Other Work-Related Mental Problems: Prevalence, Types, Assessment, and Treatment—A Scoping Review - PMC"))

The opposite is **flexible standards**:

> What is necessary for a credible draft?  
> What would be useful but optional?  
> What belongs to a later revision?  
> What is “good enough to send”?

This is not lowering your intellectual standards. It is separating **quality control** from **total control**.

## 7. Shame-based self-evaluation

Guilt says:

> “I delayed the team.”

Shame says:

> “What am I even doing?”  
> “What kind of person cannot account for a month?”  
> “Maybe I cannot trust myself.”

Your brain dump repeatedly moves from evaluating behaviour to evaluating the self. That is **global self-judgment** or shame-based self-appraisal.

The opposite is **specific, behavioural accountability**:

> “I have not given the VLM project sufficient time for four weeks. The repair is to communicate today and create the initial plan Tuesday.”

Specific accountability supports action. Global condemnation tends to produce paralysis.

## 8. Reduced self-efficacy and loss of self-trust

After repeatedly planning to finish Fedprop “this week” and not doing so, your nervous system no longer fully believes your intentions.

That is not necessarily helplessness. I would call it:

- **reduced task-specific self-efficacy**;
    
- **expectancy of non-follow-through**;
    
- or, more personally, **erosion of self-trust**.
    

Then creating another beautiful plan can feel exhausting because part of you predicts:

> “This plan will also become evidence against me.”

The opposite is not positive thinking. It is **mastery evidence**:

> Four mornings were scheduled.  
> Four mornings happened.  
> Four concrete artifacts exist.

Small kept commitments restore self-efficacy better than grand internal promises.

## 9. Psychological inflexibility and cognitive fusion

You appear somewhat **fused** with thoughts such as:

- “I should already be done.”
    
- “I should be excited.”
    
- “I must exploit this opportunity.”
    
- “I am the bottleneck.”
    
- “I cannot afford to make the wrong choice.”
    

Cognitive fusion means experiencing a thought as a command, literal truth, or definition of reality rather than as a mental event.

The opposite is **defusion and psychological flexibility**:

> “My mind is producing the thought that I should already be finished.”  
> “There is a fear that choosing Portugal will close important doors.”  
> “That fear can inform the checklist without controlling the decision.”

Psychological flexibility involves adapting behaviour in service of values while remaining open to difficult internal experiences. ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2998793/?utm_source=chatgpt.com "Psychological Flexibility as a Fundamental Aspect of Health - PMC"))

## 10. Emotional exhaustion and protective disengagement

Your “blunting” may be a form of **protective disengagement**:

> If everything matters intensely, and I cannot act on everything, the system reduces emotional responsiveness.

That is not a formal diagnosis. But it resembles aspects of occupational burnout: exhaustion, increased mental distance or negativity toward work, and reduced professional efficacy. WHO describes burnout specifically as an occupational phenomenon arising from chronic workplace stress that has not been successfully managed, rather than as a medical diagnosis. ([World Health Organization](https://www.who.int/standards/classifications/frequently-asked-questions/burn-out-an-occupational-phenomenon?utm_source=chatgpt.com "Burn-out an occupational phenomenon"))

The opposite is not immediate passion. It is **re-engagement under sustainable conditions**:

- fewer simultaneous commitments;
    
- clearer boundaries;
    
- restoration;
    
- visible completion;
    
- contact with meaning that is not contaminated by constant threat.
    

## A concise formulation of your current pattern

A therapist might write:

> Brian is facing multiple consequential, ambiguous, and partially overdue commitments during a major life transition. High personal standards and strong identification with competence amplify the perceived stakes. Uncertainty triggers anticipatory anxiety and repeated analysis. Several work tasks have become associated with guilt and self-criticism, producing task aversiveness and experiential avoidance. Avoidance gives short-term relief but increases backlog, shame, and reduced self-efficacy. Under sustained cognitive and emotional load, he experiences exhaustion, blunted motivation, and disengagement.

And the treatment direction would be:

> Reduce overload, tolerate uncertainty, separate identity from performance, approach avoided tasks in bounded doses, replace global self-criticism with behavioural accountability, and rebuild self-efficacy through repeated kept commitments.

The psychological opposite of your current state is therefore not “high motivation.” It is:

> **Psychological flexibility, uncertainty tolerance, realistic appraisal, self-compassionate accountability, and confident sequencing.**

One caution: if the emotional blunting is happening across life—not just work—and includes persistent loss of pleasure, sustained low mood, hopelessness, marked sleep or appetite changes, difficulty functioning, or thoughts of not wanting to live, it would be worth discussing with a mental-health professional rather than treating it only as a planning problem.
