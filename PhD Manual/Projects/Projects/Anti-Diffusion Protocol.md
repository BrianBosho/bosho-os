# Anti-Diffusion Protocol

Diffusion is the state where I have many interesting ideas but no deliverable.

It is distinct from burnout and isolation. It can happen even when I am excited, reading, thinking, and technically “working.”

## Warning signs

Activate this protocol if three or more are true:

- I have been thinking about an idea for more than two weeks and cannot state the research question in one sentence.
- I have read several papers but have not written a synthesis.
- I keep changing the framing of the project.
- I cannot say what the next experiment is.
- I cannot say what the expected contribution is.
- I am collecting concepts instead of producing artifacts.
- I am avoiding showing the idea because it feels too vague.
- I have more than three possible directions competing for attention.
- I feel excited but not operational.
- I cannot distinguish between “interesting” and “doable.”

## Trigger

If three or more warning signs appear, run a 48-hour Compression Sprint.

## 48-hour Compression Sprint

Within 48 hours, produce a two-page compression memo with:

1. One-sentence research question.
2. Why the question matters.
3. Three closest related papers.
4. What is missing in those papers.
5. Proposed method or analysis.
6. Dataset/model/tool needed.
7. Evaluation plan.
8. Expected claim.
9. Smallest possible experiment.
10. Person to send it to for feedback.

## Decision after compression

The idea must be assigned to one of:

1. Activate — becomes Active Project.
2. Candidate — worth developing, not primary.
3. Park — interesting, not now.
4. Kill — not worth further attention.

## Hard rule

No idea may remain between Spark and Question for more than 30 days if it is consuming serious attention.

## Forcing question

> What artifact will exist by Friday?

The artifact can be:

- project brief
- experiment table
- literature map
- code prototype
- result table
- figure
- annotated bibliography
- email to collaborator
- draft outline

If there is no artifact, it is not a project. It is intellectual fog.

## Documented case studies

These are real past failures. Use them to recognize the pattern early.

### Case 1: medVQA

**What happened:** Strong initial interest in medical visual question answering. Explored the space, read papers, had ideas. No clear research question was ever compressed. Work was done in isolation with no external feedback loop. The direction dissolved without a deliverable.

**Primary failure modes:**
- Working alone with no closed loop and no external accountability.
- Not reading deeply enough to sustain direction and productivity.
- No forcing artifact — nothing that *had* to exist by a specific date.

**Lesson:** Interest alone does not generate momentum. Momentum requires a question, a method, and a person to show it to.

### Case 2: Spectral Manifold Analysis

**What happened:** Became interested in spectral/manifold methods for analyzing representations. Explored ideas, had technical excitement. Never compressed into a research question. No deliverable emerged.

**Primary failure modes:**
- Same as medVQA: no closed loop, no artifact forcing function.
- The one time a forced deliverable existed (class project), the isolation + urgency combination produced extreme burnout rather than output.

**Lesson:** Forced deliverables without a support system are as dangerous as no deliverables. The requirement is: deliverable + feedback source + enough support to stay functional.

**What both cases had in common:**
1. Working alone for too long.
2. Not reading enough to sustain direction.
3. No concrete artifact with a deadline.
4. No person to send incomplete work to.

The Anti-Diffusion Protocol exists precisely because of these experiences.
