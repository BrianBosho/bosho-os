# CMU Ecosystem

## Purpose

Document the CMU resources — courses, research groups, and collaboration pathways — that are most relevant to the PhD direction.

CMU can provide the technical foundations that are hard to build elsewhere: rigorous courses in signal processing, information theory, multimodal ML, and theoretical ML; and access to research groups that operate at the frontier of VLM, interpretability, and safety work.

## High-priority courses

### Machine Learning for Signal Processing — 18-797 (ECE)

Data-driven approaches for characterizing signals including audio, speech, images, and video. Covers machine learning methods for signal processing problems.

**Why it matters:** The signal-processing view of neural networks and VLMs (Layer 2 of the PhD research stack) requires literacy in both ML and signal processing. This course provides exactly that bridge. Also directly relevant to the information-theoretic framing of visual evidence flow.

**Prerequisites:** Linear algebra, probability, signal processing, ML basics.  
**Source:** https://courses.ece.cmu.edu/18797

---

### Neural Computation — 15-386 (SCS)

How neurons encode and process information, adapt and learn, communicate, cooperate, compete, and compute at individual and network levels. Introduces information theory, signal processing, system analysis, statistical and probabilistic inference in the context of neural computation. Concrete examples from the visual and motor systems.

**Why it matters:** Bridges computational neuroscience, information theory, and vision — directly relevant to modeling perceptual failures in VLMs as information-processing failures. The framing of "brains making sense of inputs" maps directly onto the audacious research vision.

**Source:** https://www.csd.cmu.edu/course/15386/s26

---

### Introduction to Machine Learning — 10-301/10-601 (SCS/MLD)

Core ML foundations. Take early if needed to ensure strong theoretical grounding.

**Source:** https://www.cs.cmu.edu/~mgormley/courses/10601/schedule.html

## Collaboration strategy

The general approach for building CMU collaborations:

1. Find people doing research in areas of interest (VLM, interp, safety, info theory for ML).
2. Read their work carefully before reaching out.
3. Share a concise research direction memo or project brief.
4. Invite collaboration if there is a clear extension or shared interest.
5. Identify reading groups (VLM, interpretability, safety, ML theory) as low-friction entrypoints.

CMU collaborations built during the Pittsburgh period can be maintained during the FEUP period — the goal is to carry the same research community across both locations.

## Momentum flywheel

Reading papers consistently → gaining competence early → research advancement → supervisors granting access to programs like fellowships and internships → stronger network and industry opportunities.

The payoff on consistent paper reading is high and the cost per week is low. This is one of the highest-ROI habits to protect.

## ECE Breadth Areas

ECE has 7 technical breadth areas. The degree requires at least one graduate course from at least three. The most relevant to this research direction:

**Primary fit:**
- **Signal Processing and Communications** — directly maps to information-theoretic VLM work, Shannon analogy, Layer 2/3 research vision. Courses like 18-797 (ML for Signal Processing) likely count here.
- **Artificial Intelligence, Robotics, and Control** — VLM, interpretability, safety; most AI courses cluster here.

**Secondary fit:**
- **Algorithms, Complexity, and Programming Languages** — ML theory, information-theoretic bounds

**Others available by petition:**
- Circuits, Computer Hardware Engineering, Applied Physics, Software Systems and Computer Networking

**Strategy:** Use SPCC + AI/RC + one other. Align breadth courses with the research direction rather than treating them as a box-ticking exercise.
