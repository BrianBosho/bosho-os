## Arc 1 — "The Grounding Problem" (Research PhD narrative)

**Use for:** PhD applications, research fellowships, academic cover letters

**Core idea:**
Today's vision-language models suffer from a critical blind spot: they can achieve high benchmark accuracy while completely ignoring the visual input — relying on language priors and statistical shortcuts instead. This "illusion of competence" is dangerous in high-stakes settings. Brian's research goal is to dismantle this illusion by developing tools to measure and control visual groundedness from the inside out — treating multimodal models as information-processing chains and applying information-theoretic and spectral geometric methods to diagnose exactly where "seeing" breaks down.

**Key beats:**
1. The problem: VLMs confident and wrong — hallucinations are not bugs, they reveal architectural assumptions
2. The gap: no intrinsic diagnostic tools — current evaluation is external (benchmarks), not internal
3. Brian's approach: Visual Information Gain + Spectral Manifold Analysis of token graphs
4. Why Brian: Cryo-ET (robust perception under noise) + FedProp (spectral graph theory rigor) + RA role (daily exposure to VLM failure modes) = converging preparation
5. Why [lab/program]: [customize per application]

**Signature phrase:** "Right for the right reason — not just right."

## Arc 2 — "From Signals to Systems" (Technical depth narrative)

**Use for:** ML engineer roles, research engineer roles, applied scientist positions

**Core idea:**
Brian's path started in electrical engineering — signals, systems, linear algebra — then moved through AI master's to active research. This isn't a typical ML background. He thinks in terms of information flow, signal propagation, and mathematical guarantees — not just loss curves. That mathematical instinct is what produced FedProp's convergence proofs, and it's what now drives his spectral approach to VLM analysis. He also ships: from FastAPI backends to Next.js frontends to LangGraph agents. He can reason about the math and write the code.

**Key beats:**
1. EE foundation → AI specialization → research + production
2. FedProp: not just implemented — mathematically analyzed, proved, bounded
3. Production AI: College Agent, Colab platform — real systems, not just notebooks
4. The combination is rare: most researchers don't ship; most engineers don't prove things

## Arc 3 — "Noise, Uncertainty, and the Real World" (Applied/low-resource narrative)

**Use for:** Applied AI roles, Africa-focused programs, roles in low-resource contexts

**Core idea:**
Brian's research has consistently been shaped by real-world messiness. In Cryo-ET, images are so noisy standard models fail immediately — requiring inference from sparse, weak signals. In federated learning, data cannot be shared — requiring algorithms that work without seeing the full picture. In Kigali, sensors are expensive — so vision models must work from cheap cameras in chaotic, unstructured traffic. These contexts aren't edge cases; they're the reality of deploying AI where it matters most. Brian has learned that robust perception is not about curated benchmarks — it's about making something real work when the assumptions break down.

**Key beats:**
1. Cryo-ET: the noisiest visual data imaginable — had to infer structure from near-nothing
2. FedProp: can't share data — had to design around a hard constraint
3. Kigali traffic: LiDAR too expensive — had to make monocular cameras work
4. Thread: every project forced reliability without ideal conditions

## Arc 4 — "Research and Roots" (Africa + community narrative)

**Use for:** Africa-focused fellowships, diversity programs, community sections of applications, long-term goal statements

**Core idea:**
Brian sees research and capacity building as mutually reinforcing, not competing. His long-term goal is not just to publish papers but to help build the conditions for African researchers to do frontier AI research at home. This has already started: Local Chair for ACVSS 2025, a 10-day event connecting top African talent with global AI leaders, held in Kigali. He also mentored students at CMU-Africa. A PhD at a strong institution is the next step — not as a departure from Africa but as preparation to contribute to it more effectively.

**Key beats:**
1. The motivation is authentic — grew up in Kenya, trained at CMU-Africa, works in Kigali
2. Has already acted on it — ACVSS Local Chair, TA/mentoring at CMU-Africa
3. Not performative — the research questions themselves (low-resource AI, robust perception in African deployment contexts) are aligned
4. Long-term vision: researcher + institution builder, not just paper producer
