# Operating Manual

The operating manual translates diagnostic and identity insights into practical principles for daily and weekly living. It is not designed for an abstract productive person — it is designed for Brian specifically: ambitious, reflective, prone to overcommitment, spiritually serious, pressure-sensitive, and capable of deep work when properly contained.

The system should protect Brian from his own excesses while allowing his best qualities to come through.

## Core Principles

**1. Fewer active fronts**
Brian should almost always have fewer active fronts than he thinks he can handle.

- One primary research/work front
- One secondary support front
- One personal/health/spiritual focus
- Everything else parked

**2. Deliverables over intentions**
A day should not be defined by vague effort. It should be defined by one or two concrete outputs:
- Revise one subsection
- Produce one result table
- Run one experiment configuration
- Send one email
- Read and summarize one paper
- Complete one gym session

**3. Start small enough that the body does not revolt**
When a task is emotionally charged, the first action should be small and physical: open the document; list the missing sections; inspect the logs; write the ugly first paragraph; clean one table.

**4. Recovery is part of the plan, not a reward for finishing everything**
Brian will rarely finish everything. If rest depends on total completion, rest will never arrive. Recovery must be scheduled as infrastructure.

**5. Spiritual grounding is not optional decoration**
Meditation, Dhamma, Gita reflection, and silence are part of the system that keeps ambition from becoming bondage.

**6. The system must survive bad days**
A system that only works when Brian is inspired is not a system. The minimum viable day must be very simple.

## The Minimum Viable Day

On difficult days, execute only this:

1. Wake and do a short grounding practice
2. Identify one primary deliverable
3. Complete one focused work block
4. Move the body, even lightly
5. Capture tomorrow's first action
6. Sleep without trying to rescue the whole backlog

A day with one honest deep block, one act of bodily care, and one clean shutdown is not a failed day. It is a stabilizing day.

## Ideal Workday Structure

**Morning grounding**
- Wake. Hydrate.
- Short meditation or quiet sitting.
- Read a small passage from Dhamma, Gita, or another stabilizing source.
- Write the day's one main deliverable.

**First deep-work block** (before the world fragments the day)
- Begin with the most important task.
- Work for 90–180 minutes depending on energy.
- Produce a visible artifact.

**Midday reset**
- Eat, walk, or pause. Check whether the day is still realistic. Reduce scope if necessary.

**Second work block**
- Continue primary task or handle secondary deliverable.
- Avoid opening too many new loops.

**Late afternoon buffer**
- Meetings, admin, debugging, communication, small tasks.
- This buffer prevents the whole day from collapsing when things take longer than expected.

**Evening shutdown**
- Write what was done.
- Write tomorrow's first action.
- Mark what is postponed.
- Stop.

**Evening personal time**
- Light reading, slow walk, simple entertainment, spiritual reflection.
- Avoid turning the evening into a second anxious workday.

## Weekly Rhythm

**Monday–Thursday:** core work and research.
**Friday–Saturday:** side projects, writing, startup, creative or secondary work.
**Sunday:** life admin, weekly review, restoration, spiritual reset, personal care.

**Weekly planning ritual answers:**
1. What is the one primary outcome this week?
2. What are the two or three supporting outcomes?
3. What will I explicitly not do this week?
4. What recovery block is protected?
5. What is the first task for Monday morning?

The most important question: **"What am I not carrying this week?"**

## Research Execution Protocol

Before each research block, define:
- **Question**: What am I trying to resolve?
- **Artifact**: What will exist after this block?
- **Boundary**: When do I stop?

After each block, record:
- What changed? What evidence did I produce? What is the next action?

Break projects into 2–3 hour deliverables, not vague categories like "work on paper" or "improve experiments."

## Handling Avoidance

When avoidance appears, begin with diagnosis, not self-attack. Ask:
- Is the task unclear? Too large? Emotionally loaded?
- Am I tired? Am I afraid of what the result will imply?
- Do I need a smaller entry point?

Then use one of: 10-minute opening ritual; write the bad version; inspect, do not solve; make a list of unknowns; define the next physical action.

Avoidance is usually a signal that the task needs containment, not more pressure.

## Shutdown Ritual

Daily:
1. Write three bullets: what got done
2. Write one bullet: what remains
3. Write the first action for tomorrow
4. Explicitly postpone everything else
5. Close tabs and documents
6. Say: "The day is complete enough."

## Sunday Restoration

Sunday is for restoration and reorientation, not catch-up.

- Slow morning
- Dhamma/Gita reading
- Weekly review
- Room reset
- Walk, hike, swim, or nature time
- Light planning for the week
- No heavy work unless truly necessary
- Early sleep

The purpose is to remind Brian that he is a person before he is a producer.

## Anti-Overcommitment Rules

1. No new major project enters without naming what exits.
2. If a task cannot be scheduled, it is not yet a commitment.
3. Every weekly plan must include a "not this week" section.
4. Never plan from guilt after 9 PM.
5. Do not use Sunday night anxiety to design a heroic Monday.
6. A commitment must be sized according to actual energy, not aspirational identity.
7. Important work deserves calm, not panic.

## Crisis Protocol

When overwhelmed, do not ask: "How do I fix my whole life?"

Ask:
1. Have I eaten, slept, hydrated, and moved?
2. What is the one thing that actually matters today?
3. What can be postponed explicitly?
4. What is the next 30-minute action?
5. What would a calm serious person do now?

**Emergency reset:** shower → clean immediate space for 10 minutes → write all open loops → circle one task → do 30 minutes → stop and reassess.

## Overdue Task Protocol

1. Name the task precisely.
2. Ask whether it is still necessary.
3. Ask what the minimum acceptable version is.
4. Choose a first 30–60 minute action.
5. Decide: today, this week, or drop?

**Important:** An overdue task should not automatically receive priority. Some overdue tasks are important. Others are merely emotionally loud.

## Final Operating Statement

Brian's life system should be designed around **containment, rhythm, and purification**.

**Containment** — fewer active commitments and clearer boundaries.
**Rhythm** — repeated daily and weekly structures that reduce decision fatigue.
**Purification** — acting without letting ambition become craving, guilt, or compulsion.

The goal is not to make Brian maximally busy. The goal is to make him deeply effective, spiritually awake, physically grounded, and capable of building serious work over decades.

**The wiser inner governor says:** "Choose clearly. Act fully. Stop cleanly. Rest deliberately. Return tomorrow."
