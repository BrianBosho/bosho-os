Your **bluntness is not itself one of the kleśas**. It looks more like the resulting state when attraction, aversion, identity-pressure, and fear are all operating simultaneously.

## The central pattern

I would summarize your state as:

> **Avidyā clouds the field; asmitā makes the work personal; rāga multiplies the futures you must secure; dveṣa makes the actual tasks painful to approach; abhiniveśa supplies the underlying fear.**

### 1. Avidyā — confused or distorted seeing

This does not mean you are unintelligent or uninformed. You actually understand the projects very well.

The avidyā is in how the mind is presenting the situation:

- Everything appears simultaneously active.
- Every opportunity appears as something that must be preserved.
- Delay begins to look like permanent failure.
- The state of your work becomes confused with the state of your life.
- “I feel no excitement” begins to imply “something is fundamentally wrong.”

You are not seeing:

> “Fedprop requires four or five substantial sessions.”

You are seeing:

> “Fedprop is stale, overdue, evidence that I have wasted time, and possibly evidence that I cannot rely on myself.”

That additional colouring is the kleśa.

**Its opposite is viveka—discrimination or clear seeing:**

- this is urgent; that is merely attractive;
- this is today’s task; that is next month’s possibility;
- this project is delayed; _I_ am not a delayed human being;
- this decision has uncertainty; it does not require perfect certainty.

### 2. Asmitā — identification with the worker and performer

This is especially visible in phrases such as:

> “What am I even doing?”
> “I cannot account for my time.”
> “I am the bottleneck.”
> “I should feel excited about this.”

The projects are not merely projects. They have become evidence about **who Brian is**:

- Am I disciplined?
- Am I a serious researcher?
- Can people depend on me?
- Am I wasting my opportunities?
- Am I becoming the person I thought I would become?

That is asmitā: identification with the mind, role, performance, and story of “me.”

**Its opposite is witnessing and non-identification:**

> “There is guilt.”
> “There is avoidance.”
> “The paper has been delayed.”
> “The mind is frightened about what this means.”

Rather than:

> “I am a failure who cannot follow through.”

The facts can still be taken seriously without turning them into identity.

### 3. Rāga — attachment to desirable outcomes

Your brain dump contains many genuinely attractive possibilities:

- complete Fedprop;
- publish the VLM paper in July;
- choose the best possible PhD opening;
- benefit from Jaime’s supervision;
- access CMU’s courses and ecosystem;
- preserve internship opportunities;
- build a hobby app that may generate income;
- make the most of every available opportunity.

None of these desires is inherently wrong. The kleśa appears when the mind says:

> “I must preserve all of these possible futures.”

You then become unable to choose because choosing one path temporarily excludes another.

**Its opposite is vairāgya—non-attachment.** That does not mean not caring. It means caring and acting without demanding that every attractive outcome remain available. Yoga traditionally pairs repeated practice with non-attachment as complementary ways of steadying the mind. ([swamij.com](https://swamij.com/glossary/vairagya.htm?utm_source=chatgpt.com "Vairagya - Yoga Meditation Glossary"))

For you, this might sound like:

> “The app is attractive, but it is not active this week.”
> “Portugal may be good without being perfect.”
> “Fedprop can be submitted without containing every possible experiment.”

### 4. Dveṣa — aversion

This may be the most visible kleśa at the task level.

Fedprop has become associated with:

- staleness;
- guilt;
- disappointment;
- repeated failed plans;
- fear of inspecting the actual state;
- anticipated criticism;
- uncertainty about how much remains.

Planning itself has also become aversive because it makes you contact all the accumulated obligations.

Therefore, when you approach the project, the mind does not merely encounter a results section. It encounters months of unpleasant emotional conditioning. It recoils.

This explains the strange gap between:

> “Objectively, this is a valuable opportunity and I have support.”

and:

> “I feel no eagerness to touch it.”

**The opposite is not excitement. It is willingness or equanimity:**

> “This feels stale and unpleasant, and I can still work on one table for ninety minutes.”

That is a much more attainable state than trying to force yourself to love the paper again.

### 5. Abhiniveśa — clinging, insecurity, fear of loss

Under the indifference, you already sensed a “store of fear.”

The brain dump contains fear of:

- losing time;
- missing the visa window;
- choosing the wrong country;
- beginning the PhD badly;
- missing foundational training;
- disappointing collaborators;
- losing publication opportunities;
- being financially constrained;
- discovering that you have not used the past month well.

In the classical framework, abhiniveśa is the deep clinging to continuity, security, and preservation of the self. In your current life, it does not need to appear as literal fear of death. It appears more practically as:

> “My future may be damaged if I do not get this right.”

**Its opposite is not recklessness.** It is courage and acceptance of irreducible uncertainty:

> “I will preserve the options I reasonably can, gather the important information, and then choose. I cannot construct a future with no possibility of regret.”

## Why these particular kleśas are strong now

I think several conditions have reinforced them.

**Your work is strongly linked to identity.** Research output, leadership, competence, and future impact matter deeply to you. That makes asmitā especially easy to activate.

**You have several real opportunities at once.** This strengthens rāga—the urge to maximize them all rather than accept sequencing and exclusion.

**Some projects have remained open for too long.** Repeated guilt, avoidance, and failed completion promises condition dveṣa. The project itself begins to feel unpleasant before you even open the document.

**You are entering a high-stakes transition.** Visa decisions, Portugal versus Pittsburgh, finances, supervisors, and the beginning of a PhD naturally activate abhiniveśa.

**Exhaustion has reduced discrimination.** When the mind is rested, it can distinguish “urgent administrative task” from “interesting future project.” When depleted, everything becomes one threatening object. That is how avidyā governs the whole field.

## The loop visible in the brain dump

It seems to run like this:

> **Rāga:** I must make the most of every opportunity.
> **Asmitā:** My ability to do this proves what kind of person I am.
> **Abhiniveśa:** If I fail, I may damage my future.
> **Dveṣa:** Contact with the work becomes painful, so I withdraw.
> **Avidyā:** The distinct problems collapse into one enormous problem.
> **Blunting:** I feel nothing because feeling everything would be intolerable.

So the indifference is not the absence of caring. It may be what caring looks like after it has become excessively coloured by fear, identity, attraction, and aversion.

## What is the opposite overall state?

Not excitement.

The opposite is an **akliṣṭa**, relatively uncoloured state: the facts remain, but they are not saturated with attraction, aversion, fear, or identity. Traditional explanations contrast kliṣṭa thoughts—coloured by attachment, aversion, fear, egoism, or ignorance—with akliṣṭa thoughts that permit freer choice. ([swamij.com](https://swamij.com/klishta-aklishta.htm?utm_source=chatgpt.com "Uncoloring Your Colored Thoughts through Yoga"))

It would sound like this:

> Fedprop needs four mornings.
> The VLM team needs a plan and an initial contribution.
> The visa option should be preserved while I gather Portugal information.
> The app is not active this week.
> My current exhaustion is a condition to work with, not a verdict about me.

That state is **clear, sober, bounded, and steady**. It may not feel inspiring. But it contains agency.

Useful labels for the moment they arise:

- **Avidyā:** “I am seeing the entire future instead of the present fact.”
- **Asmitā:** “I have turned this task into a statement about myself.”
- **Rāga:** “I am trying to retain every attractive possibility.”
- **Dveṣa:** “I am recoiling from the discomfort associated with this work.”
- **Abhiniveśa:** “My mind is treating uncertainty as a threat to my future.”

The practice is not to condemn the kleśa. It is simply to recognize: **“This thought is coloured.”** That recognition already begins to loosen it.
