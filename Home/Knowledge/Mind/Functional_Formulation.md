# Functional Formulation

## Function

A psychological map of the current pattern — not a diagnosis. Instead of asking "what is wrong with me?", it asks: *What is happening internally, what triggers it, what maintains it, and what function does the apparent indifference serve?* Use it to name a mechanism in the moment and reach for its opposite.

This is the clinical/ACT-CBT lens. The yogic lens on the same state is [[Five_Kleshas]]; the attention/agency lens is [[Mind_Attention_Environment]]. The container that absorbs the load is [[Default_Week_Architecture]].

## The pattern in one paragraph

Multiple consequential, ambiguous, partially overdue commitments collide during a major life transition. High personal standards and strong identification with competence amplify the stakes. Uncertainty triggers anticipatory anxiety and repeated analysis. Several tasks (Fedprop especially) have become associated with guilt and self-criticism, so they feel aversive; avoidance gives short-term relief but increases backlog, shame, and erosion of self-trust. Under sustained load the system blunts — emotional responsiveness drops because feeling everything at full intensity would be intolerable. The indifference is not absence of caring; it is caring after it has been over-coloured by fear, identity, and aversion.

The opposite is not "more motivation." It is **psychological flexibility, uncertainty tolerance, realistic appraisal, self-compassionate accountability, and confident sequencing.**

## The ten mechanisms and their antidotes

1. **Role / cognitive overload** — carrying author, research lead, collaborator, future PhD student, logistics manager, decision-maker, prospective founder at once. → *Externalized structure and role containment:* "From 8–11 I am only the Fedprop author." Fixed blocks relieve precisely because they remove competing identities.
2. **Anticipatory anxiety** — anxiety from uncertain possible outcomes, not a present catastrophe. → *Realistic threat appraisal:* What exactly is the threat? How likely? What action reduces it? What can't be controlled today? (The visa is not "my future is in danger"; it is "I have an Aug 18 appointment and need to investigate an earlier interview.")
3. **Intolerance of uncertainty** — uncertainty itself becomes so aversive that you re-analyze, delay, and struggle to commit (the Portugal decision). → *Uncertainty tolerance:* collect decision-relevant information, set a deadline, choose without requiring complete certainty.
4. **Experiential avoidance** — disengaging, lying down, or switching to a novel app idea to escape guilt/fear/self-criticism the task evokes. → *Willingness:* "I am willing to feel guilt and resistance while rewriting this one table." Not excitement — willingness.
5. **Avoidance learning / task aversiveness** — Fedprop conditioned as aversive through a negative-reinforcement loop (think → tension → postpone → relief → more threatening next time). → *Graded approach:* open the paper, work a tightly bounded unit, tolerate the activation, stop at a defined point, repeat until contact is less threatening.
6. **Maladaptive perfectionism / overcontrol** — standards become "make the best PhD choice, use every opportunity, make the paper comprehensive, feel the correct emotion." → *Flexible standards:* separate **quality control** from **total control**. What's necessary for a credible draft? What's optional? What's "good enough to send"?
7. **Shame-based self-evaluation** — moving from "I delayed the team" (guilt) to "what kind of person can't account for a month?" (shame). → *Specific behavioural accountability:* "I have not given VLM enough time for four weeks; the repair is to communicate today and plan Tuesday." Global condemnation produces paralysis.
8. **Reduced self-efficacy / loss of self-trust** — after repeatedly planning to "finish this week" and not, the nervous system stops believing intentions. → *Mastery evidence:* four mornings scheduled, four mornings happened, four concrete artifacts exist. Small kept commitments restore trust better than grand internal promises.
9. **Psychological inflexibility / cognitive fusion** — experiencing thoughts ("I should already be done," "I am the bottleneck") as literal commands. → *Defusion:* "My mind is producing the thought that I should be finished." The fear can inform the checklist without controlling the decision.
10. **Emotional exhaustion / protective disengagement** — burnout-like blunting from chronic unmanaged load. → *Re-engagement under sustainable conditions:* fewer simultaneous commitments, clearer boundaries, restoration, visible completion, contact with meaning not contaminated by threat.

## Treatment direction

Reduce overload, tolerate uncertainty, separate identity from performance, approach avoided tasks in bounded doses, replace global self-criticism with behavioural accountability, and rebuild self-efficacy through repeated kept commitments.

## Guardrail

If the blunting spreads across all of life — persistent loss of pleasure, sustained low mood, hopelessness, marked sleep/appetite change, difficulty functioning, or thoughts of not wanting to live — treat it as more than a planning problem and discuss it with a mental-health professional.
